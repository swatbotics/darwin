Experimenting with DARwIn: Part 3 Joint Layout and Kinemetics
Written by i-Bot   

This article documents my findings on the joint layout and kinematics of the DARwin robot and compares with other robots from Robotis and other manufacturers. The objective is to use the DARwIn software framework with other humanoid robots.

i-Bot a.k.a. Richard Ibbotson July 2011

Orientation

To understand the DARwin layout and to make the descriptions, I needed to have a reference for directions.

First for the Robotis MX28 servo:

























This appears to be standard for all Robotis servos. The MX servo position values range from 0 to 4095 for the latest high resolution servo firmware with a maximum +/- 180 degree range. Other Robotis servos position values range from 0 to 1023 with a maximum +/- 150 degree range. The actual operating angle of the servo is usually constrained to less than the above range due to mechanical constraints of the joints and body.

　

It is not standard for servos from other manufacturers, for instance the wCK servo from Robobuilder has this reversed layout:























For the complete DARwin robot the reference frame is:








































DARwin-OP Joint Layout.






















































The DARwIn-OP leg joint layout is similar to that of most small humanoid robots. The two sets of three servos (7,9,11 and 8,10,12) have their z axis aligned through a common point and create a spherical hip joint around that common point. The two knee joints (13 and 14) allow the length of the leg to be changed. Finally a 2 degree of freedom ankle is added based on the two servo pairs (15,17 and 16,18).

The arms each have 3 degrees of freedom and are configured to allow the robot to swing the arms and to get up from a fallen position. There is little provision in the design for dextrous use of the arms.

Finally the head is equipped with pan and tilt for the head mounted camera and to provide some simple visual feedback by simple movements (nod and shake).


Comparison to Bioloid Premium Humanoid Joint Layout

As can be seen there are few differences to the joint layout of the DARwin OP and the Humanoid Type A. They are:

  • The humanoid Type A has no head
  • The elbow joints(5 and 6) are rotated by 90 degrees
  • Some Servos are either reversed or mounted at a different angle
However the basic configuration of the hip, knee, ankle and shoulder is the same.


Comparison to other Humanoids

When comparing to other humanoids, the greatest difference is in the hip configuration. Some humanoids (Manoi, Robonova, Bioloid-GP, Robobuilder) do not have a hip yaw servo in their standard build. Also the servo alignment in the hip and ankle may not provide a simple spherical or circular joint.

Modular construction of most humanoid robots does allow them to be rebuilt into a close copy of the DARwin-OP joint layout.

Below is my own DARwin build of the Robobuilder. Note the servo numbers are very different and conform to the Robobuilder numbering and not DARwin. This simplifies the reuse of Robobuilder .rbm files for motions.

How Much Does Joint Layout Matter ?

Obviously to minimise changes to the DARwin framework it is best to be as close to the DARwin-OP layout as possible. However humanoids such as the Bioloid GP have good performance while missing a degree of freedom in the hip and would simplify the IK caclulations.

The Robotis DARwin software framework uses motion modules to control the servos and there are currently 3 of these (head, walking, and action). The head is most simple and controls the head pan and tilt with the camera, and can be left out if necessary.

Walking uses an algorithmic walking gait which moves the position and orientation of the feet in relation to the body. Inverse kinematics is used to determine the leg servo positions to control the position and orientation of the foot. The inverse kinematics is part defined in variable and part hard coded, so being close to the DARwin-OP joint layout helps.

Action plays existing motions developed or captured using Roboplus or similar. Keeping the joint layout close to existing standard humanoid configurations allows maximum reuse of existing motions.

Walking and Action will likely make conflicting demands on joint layout in alternative DARwin builds. The important thing seems to be able to see where alternative build differs, and to be able to describe this geometrically.


The DARwin framework demonstrates the current crossover taking place, from captured and hand crafted motions, to a more algorithmic approach. Internal use in software of angles and kinematics, rather than position values for most servo calculations makes development and simulation easier, but this does usually mean floating point calculations. Moving to more complex software such as the UPenn code will make rigorous analysis and description of joints even more important.

Conclusions:

This analysis suggests it is not too difficult to use the DARwIn software frames work on other Robotis humanoids, and humanoids from other manufacturers. This is confirmed by subsequent successful porting to the Robobuilder 20 DOF humanoid.

I have always found it difficult to think and work in multiple spacial frames, so this article is as much a description for me as well as others. If I have made mistakes, then please correct me.

i-Bot a.k.a. Richard Ibbotson July 2011
